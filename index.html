Elstob a variable font for medievalists

The Elstob font aims to include all Unicode characters commonly used by medievalists. It is a variable font—that is, one that can do the work of a number of conventional fonts by varying different aspects of the characters’ shapes. Variable fonts are especially appropriate for use on the web, since a single file can do the work of multiple files, with significant speed gains. All the major browsers now support variable fonts: if this page doesn't work for you, consider updating your browser.

To get a sense of what Elstob can do, play with the sliders and checkboxes below, and check out the notes on the font’s variable axes at the bottom of this page. The sample paragraphs are editable: type or paste in a bit of your favorite text to see what it looks like in Elstob.

This is a work in progress. To do: lots of work on spacing, especially in the italic face; further refinement of OpenType features; fixing of ligature widths; check for additional medieval characters. Is hinting of a variable font possible yet? Is it necessary?

Size: 20
Weight: 380
Optical Size: 60
Grade: 1

This is Elstob roman. The font is based on the Double Pica commissioned by John Fell (1625–1686) for the use of the Oxford University Press. Wherever possible, the type is based on a specimen book printed in 1925 with type cast in the nineteenth century from the seventeenth-century matrices; digital images from the 1693 and 1706 Fell specimen books served as backup, and also an early eighteenth-century folio in which a lengthy dedication was printed in Fell Double Pica. The typeface doesn’t have a high reputation: the typographer Stanley Morison called it “amateurish.” However, its angular character (especially its flat or flattish serifs with minimal or no brackets) makes it well suited to adaptation as a variable font. Elstob attempts to improve on the original face by removing idiosyncrasies and correcting awkward and erroneous features.

Size: 20

Slant: 0

The font is named for Elizabeth Elstob (1683–1756), a major scholar of the English language as recorded before the Norman Conquest (1066). Although her published works do not use Fell Double Pica, her close association with the Oxford “Saxonists” of her day (see the Oxford Dictionary of National Biography entry) makes the name appropriate. The Fell Double Pica italic has a pointed look that, in Elstob, becomes more pronounced as the slant increases (the slant number decreases). For the closest approximation of the original roman and italic, set the sliders to Weight 380 and Optical Size 100, then Grade 1 for roman and Slant -40 for italic.

Notes on the Variable Axes

An axis is an aspect of a font that can be varied along a numerical range. A web designer can either choose some combination of axes for static text or vary the axes dynamically (for example, changing the weight of a stretch of type in response to some kind of user action). Each of Elstob’s two faces has three axes: Both roman and italic have Weight and Optical Size; additionally, the roman face has Grade and the italic has Slant.

Weight varies the heaviness of the font along a numerical axis from 300 to 800, where 300 is “Light” and 800 is “Extra Bold.” There are several standard weights between these extremes: “Regular” 380 (or 400 if you prefer the standard numerical scheme), “Medium” 500, “Semibold” 600, “Bold” 700. However, Weight can be varied continuously along the 500-unit axis.

Optical Size optimizes the type for display at different sizes by varying the x-height, the contrast, and the width of thin strokes. Smaller numbers are more appropriate for smaller type. Elstob names certain points on the Optical Size axis: “Fine Print” 1 (best at around 6 points); “Footnote” 20 (8 points), “Block Quote” 40 (10 points), “Regular” 60 (12 points), “Subhead” 80 (14 points), “Display” 100 (16 points or larger). However, there’s nothing to stop your using any Optical Size you like at any actual size, if you like the look of it.

Grade is like Weight in that it varies the heaviness of the letters; but it does so without changing their width. Use Grade when you want to change the weight of a stretch of text dynamically (for example, on mouseover) without annoying your readers by forcing the text to reflow. The range of allowable numbers on this axis is 1–500.

Slant is the amount of slope in an italic typeface: in Elstob Italic it runs from -40 (most slanted) to 60 (least slanted). “Normal” for Elstob is zero, though the slant of the original Fell italic is -40. Use higher numbers for a more modern look.
